Deconstructing Gross Domestic Product (GDP)

GDP is the total value of everything produced in an economy. It can be measured by what we spend (the Expenditure Approach) or what we earn (the Income Approach). Both methods give us the same total, revealing the core structure of our economy.

GDP by Expenditure (What We Buy)

Personal Consumption is the largest driver of the economy, accounting for nearly 60% of all spending.

GDP by Income (What We Earn)

Wages and salaries represent the largest share of income generated from production, highlighting the importance of labour in the economy.

The Human Element: Inflation & Unemployment

Price stability and full employment are two of the most important economic goals. Inflation erodes purchasing power, while unemployment represents lost output and personal hardship. Policymakers constantly navigate the trade-offs between them.

Understanding Unemployment

Unemployment isn't monolithic. It's composed of different types, some of which are natural in a dynamic economy (Frictional, Structural), while others signal economic downturns (Cyclical).

The Inflation-Unemployment Trade-off

The Phillips Curve illustrates a short-run inverse relationship: policies that lower unemployment often lead to higher inflation, and vice-versa. In the long run, however, there is no such trade-off.

Steering the Economy: Policy Levers

When the economy deviates from its potential, the government and the central bank use fiscal and monetary policy to steer it back on course. These tools are used to manage aggregate demand, fighting recessions and inflation.

Fiscal Policy: The Government's Toolkit

The government uses spending and taxes to influence the economy.
